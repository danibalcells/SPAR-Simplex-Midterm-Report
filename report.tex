\begin{abstract}
Recent work has established that toy transformers trained on emission data from hidden Markov models (HMMs) linearly encode the current belief state---the posterior distribution over hidden states---in their residual stream~\cite{shai2024}. In this work, we investigate whether this phenomenon arises in-context in a production-scale language model without any additional fine-tuning. Using Llama-3.2-3B prompted with sequences from the Mess3 process, we find that the model reaches near-Bayesian-optimal next-token predictions after roughly 23 tokens, and that linear probes trained on residual stream activations recover the belief state with $R^2 > 0.998$ at mid-early layers. We also present preliminary evidence that the belief state remains linearly decodable even for processes where the mapping from belief states to output probabilities is non-injective, suggesting the model tracks genuine latent structure rather than merely compressing its output distribution. Beyond these correlational findings, we show that the representations are causally relevant: both activation patching and activation steering shift the model's output distribution in the direction of a target belief state. Tuned Lens analysis reveals that earlier layers are more predictive of ground truth HMM probabilities. Further, early results suggest that belief geometry formation may proceed via \emph{constrained belief updating}, an additive approximation of true Bayesian inference, though this requires further investigation. We synthesize these findings into a picture in which the model constructs a linear belief geometry in early layers, then routes that belief to its output through a distinct mechanism in later layers. Future work will investigate the mechanisms underlying belief geometry formation and output routing in detail.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A central question about large language models is whether they merely learn surface-level statistical regularities or whether they develop structured internal models of the processes that generate their inputs. Hidden Markov models (HMMs) offer a controlled setting in which to test this: an optimal predictor of HMM-generated sequences must maintain a \emph{belief state}---the posterior distribution over hidden states given the observed history---whereas a model that merely compresses token co-occurrence statistics need not. Shai et al.~\cite{shai2024} showed that toy transformers trained on HMM emission data do in fact linearly encode this Bayesian-optimal belief state geometry in their residual stream. Whether the same holds for large, general-purpose language models processing text in context remains an open question.


Park et al.~\cite{park2025} provided complementary evidence that LLMs prompted with random walks on a fixed graph reorganize their internal activations to track the graph structure rather than the surface token identities. We build on both lines of work by asking whether large language models, when prompted with sequences from an HMM, develop internal representations of the underlying belief state geometry purely in context.

This project investigates four related questions: (1) Can Llama-3.2-3B, prompted with a long sequence from an HMM, approach Bayesian-optimal predictions in context? (2) If so, is the current belief state represented approximately linearly in the residual stream? (3) If so, is this representation used causally, in the sense that modifying it shifts the model's output distribution in a predictable way? (4) How does this representation emerge across layers and context positions, and how is it used to determine output token probabilities?

Developing a principled, theoretically-grounded understanding of the computational structures that arise in large language models during pretraining is of broad relevance to AI safety. Without a clear account of the underlying structures that LLMs build and how those structures relate to their training data and behavior, it is difficult to interpret or trust these models at scale. Establishing what representations LLMs form, how those representations are constructed from context, and how they influence model outputs contributes to the rigorous understanding of LLM behavior that is necessary for anticipating failure modes, understanding what these models have learned, and informing safety-relevant interventions.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

We use Llama-3.2-3B~\cite{llama32}, a 28-layer base model with no instruction tuning, to avoid interference from fine-tuning objectives. Activations are extracted via TransformerLens at the output of each residual stream layer.

As the generative process we use Mess3, a three-state HMM over tokens $\{A, B, C\}$ with parameters $x = 0.05$ and $\alpha = 0.9$. The belief state at each sequence position is the posterior distribution over the three hidden states $\mathbf{b}_t = (b_t^{(1)}, b_t^{(2)}, b_t^{(3)})$, which lies on the 2-simplex $\Delta^2 = \{\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^3 : b^{(i)} \geq 0,\, \sum_i b^{(i)} = 1\}$. Sequences of up to 5,000 tokens are generated per run. Figure~\ref{fig:optimal_belief_simplex} shows the resulting belief geometry: as the observer updates its posterior given each new token, the belief state traces out a characteristic three-pronged structure on the simplex, with high-density clusters near each vertex corresponding to high certainty about the current hidden state.

\subsection{In-context HMM prediction}

\paragraph{Interfacing HMM tokens with the LLM.}
The model's tokenizer does not treat bare single characters and space-prefixed characters identically: the first token in a sequence is encoded as a bare character (e.g.\ \texttt{A}), while all subsequent tokens are encoded as space-prefixed characters (\texttt{{\char`\ }A}, \texttt{{\char`\ }B}, \texttt{{\char`\ }C}), each of which maps to a single entry in the model's vocabulary. At each sequence position $t \geq 1$, we extract the model's logits over the full vocabulary and retain only the three entries corresponding to \texttt{{\char`\ }A}, \texttt{{\char`\ }B}, and \texttt{{\char`\ }C}. The remaining probability mass---assigned to all other vocabulary entries---constitutes a ``junk'' probability that the model places on tokens outside the HMM alphabet.

\paragraph{KL divergence and junk mass.}
To assess whether the model learns to predict Mess3 sequences in context, we measure the KL divergence between the Bayesian-optimal next-token distribution $\mathbf{p}$ and the model's output distribution $\mathbf{q}$ at each position:
\[
  D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf{p} \,\|\, \mathbf{q})
  = \sum_{k \in \{A,B,C\}} p_k \log \frac{p_k}{q_k}\,.
\]
Because the optimal distribution assigns zero probability to tokens outside $\{A,B,C\}$, the junk term $p_{\text{junk}} \log(p_{\text{junk}} / q_{\text{junk}})$ vanishes and the KL can be computed using only the three HMM token probabilities without renormalization. Note, however, that junk mass still affects the divergence indirectly: probability assigned to non-HMM tokens reduces $q_A + q_B + q_C$ below~1, increasing the KL.

\paragraph{Convergence criterion.}
We define a convergence threshold at 2\% of the maximum KL, smoothed over a 5-token window with a minimum position of 20, and record the token position at which this is first crossed.

\subsection{Linear decodability of belief states}
\label{sec:linear_decodability}

\paragraph{Mess3.}
We extract residual stream activations at each layer and train linear probes (ordinary least squares) to predict the three-dimensional belief state vector from activations. Probes are trained pooled across multiple sequences: 10 training sequences and 5 held-out evaluation sequences, each 5,000 tokens long. We report mean squared error (MSE) and the coefficient of determination $R^2$, evaluated on the held-out sequences.

We also train a paired encoder-decoder: the encoder is a linear map from activations to a learned latent space, and the decoder maps from that latent space back to token probabilities. This gives a complementary measure of how much information relevant to the model's output is mediated through a low-dimensional linear subspace of the residual stream.

\paragraph{Cross-sequence consistency.}
To assess whether different sequences induce similar belief-state representations, we also train separate probes on individual sequences and compare them pairwise. For each layer, we compute the cosine similarity between per-sequence probe weight matrices, the first principal angle $\theta_1$ between the column spaces they span, and the cross-MSE ratio (the MSE when one sequence's probe is applied to another sequence's activations, divided by the self-evaluation MSE). We also compare the pooled probe to each per-sequence probe using the same metrics.

\paragraph{Fern and Leopard.}
A concern with Mess3 is that the emission matrix defines a linear map from the belief state to the output token distribution. This means that a high-quality linear probe could in principle reflect a linear encoding of the model's current output probabilities rather than the belief state proper. In that case, the representation would not require any latent-state tracking beyond what is needed to produce the current output.

The Fern process addresses this ambiguity. It has two output tokens and three hidden states, with an emission matrix that does not admit a left inverse, so the output distribution does not uniquely determine the belief state. Fern is parameterized by a single parameter $x \in [0, 1]$ that controls the coupling between states 1 and 2: the transition matrices interpolate between symmetric ($x = 0$) and maximally asymmetric ($x = 1$) configurations. We test $x \in \{0.4, 0.63\}$. If the model still encodes a three-dimensional belief state geometry for Fern, this provides stronger evidence that it tracks latent states rather than simply compressing its output distribution.

We additionally test the Leopard process, another 3-state, 2-token HMM parameterized by $x \in [0, 1]$, with a sparser, quasi-deterministic transition structure (many zero entries). Leopard tests whether the model can track belief states for processes with sharply different mixing properties. We test $x \in \{0.15, 0.5\}$.

\paragraph{Belief-state sweep.}
To systematically assess how HMM structure affects belief-state encoding, we run a sweep across all three process families (Mess3, Fern, Leopard) and multiple parameterizations, totaling 8 configurations. For each configuration, we generate 50 sequences of length 1000 tokens (20 train, 30 test), extract residual stream activations at all 28 layers, train linear probes (ordinary least squares) to decode the 3D belief-state vector, visualize the resulting belief-state geometry on the 2-simplex via barycentric projection, and visualize the residual-stream activation geometry via 2D PCA projections colored by belief state.

\paragraph{Emergence.}
To study how the belief state representation develops over the course of a sequence, we track the token position at which the probe's $R^2$ first crosses 0.9 at each layer, and compare this to the position at which the model's KL divergence converges. A negative lag (probe threshold reached before KL convergence) indicates that a given layer begins encoding the belief state before the model's output distribution has fully adapted.

\subsection{Causal use of belief states}

\paragraph{Activation patching.}
To test whether the belief state representation is causally relevant, we intervene on the residual stream at the last token position of each sequence. For each sequence, we compute a target belief state $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$, map it to an activation vector via the decoder trained in the linear decodability experiments, and wholesale replace the residual stream at the intervention position with this decoded activation. We then measure the KL divergence between the model's output distribution and the Bayesian-optimal distribution implied by $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$.

We evaluate four intervention conditions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Factual}: $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is the Bayesian-optimal belief after the full observed prefix, including the actual last token.
  \item \textbf{Counterfactual}: the sequence is identical except at the final token, where we substitute one of the two alternative tokens. $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is the belief that would result from a single HMM transition step with this alternative token, starting from the belief state just before the final position. This yields two counterfactual specs per sequence.
  \item \textbf{Garbage valid}: $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is the end-of-sequence belief from a different sequence---a point that is reachable on the simplex for the Mess3 process but unrelated to the current sequence's context.
  \item \textbf{Garbage random}: $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}$ is sampled uniformly from the 2-simplex via $\mathrm{Dirichlet}(1,1,1)$.
\end{itemize}

A key hypothesis is that the factual and counterfactual conditions should yield similar patching performance, because the prefix up to position $L{-}1$ is shared and attention at the intervention position pulls information from the same context in both cases. Any discrepancy would therefore require a different explanation.

\paragraph{Activation steering.}
Activation patching replaces the entire residual stream vector with the decoder output, destroying any information outside of the subspace spanned by the decoder weights. Activation steering is a lighter-touch intervention: instead of replacing the activation, we \emph{add} a steering vector that modifies only the belief-relevant subspace while preserving all other information in the residual stream. For a given layer~$L$, the steering vector is
\[
  \mathbf{v}_L = D_L(\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}) - D_L(\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{source}})\,,
\]
where $D_L$ is the affine decoder trained in the linear decodability experiments (Section~\ref{sec:linear_decodability}). Since $D_L(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = W_{\mathrm{dec}}\boldsymbol{\eta} + \mathbf{b}_{\mathrm{dec}}$, the bias cancels and the steering vector reduces to $\mathbf{v}_L = W_{\mathrm{dec}}(\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}} - \boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{source}})$: the decoder's image of the belief-space displacement. The steered activation is $\mathbf{a}_{\mathrm{steered}} = \mathbf{a}_{\mathrm{original}} + \mathbf{v}_L$, applied via a hook at \texttt{blocks.\{L\}.hook\_resid\_post}. Comparing steering to patching reveals whether information outside the decoder subspace is computationally relevant: if steering outperforms patching, the non-decoder-subspace information matters and the decoder alone does not capture the full representation; if the two perform similarly, the decoder subspace is sufficient.

The steering intervention is applied at the last $K$ token positions simultaneously: we compute a steering vector $\mathbf{v}_L(t)$ at each position and add all $K$ vectors in a single forward pass. This tests whether the model's belief representation is causally active across token positions, not just at the final position. We evaluate four conditions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Past-consistent (different-prefix)}: For each source sequence~$X$ of length~$T$, pick a donor HMM sequence~$Z$ generated independently. Replace the last $K$ tokens of~$X$ with the last $K$ tokens of~$Z$, forming a hybrid prefix. At each modified position $t \in \{T{-}K, \ldots, T{-}1\}$, compute the target belief $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}(t)$ under the hybrid prefix using forward filtering, and steer toward it. This tests whether the model can be redirected toward a belief trajectory that is \emph{internally consistent} (each target belief follows from the previous one via a valid HMM update) but diverges from the source sequence.
  \item \textbf{Past-consistent (shared-prefix)}: Identical to the above, except the donor continuation is \emph{sampled from the HMM starting at the source sequence's belief at position $T{-}K$}. Source and donor share the same belief history up to the split point, isolating the pure belief-injection effect from any prefix-attention conflict.
  \item \textbf{Garbage valid}: Steer toward a donor sequence's final belief, applied only at the final position. The target is a valid Mess3 belief but unrelated to the source context.
  \item \textbf{Garbage random}: Steer toward a random point on the 2-simplex sampled from $\mathrm{Dirichlet}(1,1,1)$, applied only at the final position.
\end{itemize}
We test $K \in \{1, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100\}$ for the past-consistent conditions. For each condition, we report $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf{q}_{\mathrm{steered}} \,\|\, \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{opt}}(\boldsymbol{\eta}_{\mathrm{target}}))$---the KL divergence between the steered output distribution and the Bayesian-optimal distribution implied by the target belief.

\paragraph{Tuned lens.}
To assess the predictive power of intermediate representations, we need a principled way to extract next-token predictions from each layer's residual stream. The logit lens---directly applying the final layer norm and unembedding matrix to intermediate activations---is not well-suited for this, because the residual stream representation can undergo substantial rotations and rescalings across layers, so projecting an early-layer activation onto the final unembedding may not reflect what information that layer actually encodes. The tuned lens~\cite{tunedlens} addresses this by training a per-layer affine mapping that translates each layer's activations into the final layer's coordinate system before applying the unembedding, providing a more faithful readout of each layer's predictive content.

For each layer~$l$, the tuned lens applies an identity-initialized affine transformation $T_l$ to the residual stream activation $\mathbf{h}_l$ before the final layer norm and unembedding. We train two variants:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Model-target:} Trained to minimize $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{final}} \,\|\, \mathrm{softmax}(T_l(\mathbf{h}_l) \cdot W_{\mathrm{concept}} + \mathbf{b}_{\mathrm{concept}}))$, where $\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{final}}$ is the model's final-layer output distribution over concept tokens.
  \item \textbf{HMM-target:} Trained with the same architecture but targeting the HMM's observation probabilities (ground-truth next-token distribution given the belief state).
\end{itemize}
This yields four KL divergence metrics per layer: $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathrm{HMM} \,\|\, \mathrm{tuned\_hmm})$, $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathrm{model} \,\|\, \mathrm{tuned\_model})$, $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathrm{HMM} \,\|\, \mathrm{tuned\_model})$, and $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathrm{model} \,\|\, \mathrm{tuned\_hmm})$. By plotting these against the linear probe $R^2$ at each layer, we can disentangle how well each layer's activations encode the HMM belief state versus the model's own output distribution, and whether the layers that most faithfully encode the belief state are also the layers whose representations are most directly useful for prediction.

\paragraph{Later-layer computation.}
\label{sec:later_layer_method}
To characterize what information later layers encode beyond the belief state, we decompose residual stream activations into belief-aligned and orthogonal components and train separate decoders on each. At each layer, we fit a linear regression from activations to the HMM belief-state vector (the same probe used in Section~\ref{sec:linear_decodability}). The probe weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{\mathrm{states}} \times d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ defines a low-dimensional subspace in activation space; we obtain an orthonormal basis $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}} \times n_{\mathrm{states}}}$ for this subspace via QR decomposition of $W^\top$. The belief-aligned component of an activation $\mathbf{h}$ is then $\mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{belief}} = (Q Q^\top) \mathbf{h}$, and the orthogonal component is $\mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{orth}} = \mathbf{h} - \mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{belief}}$. We report the fraction of total activation variance captured by each component across layers.

We then train Ridge regression decoders ($\alpha = 1$) from three input representations to multiple targets (concept logits, HMM next-token distribution, belief entropy): (i)~belief coordinates $\mathbf{h} Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{\mathrm{states}}}$, (ii)~PCA of $\mathbf{h}_{\mathrm{orth}}$ with $n_{\mathrm{states}}$ components (dimensionality-matched to the belief subspace as a control), and (iii)~their concatenation. All decoders share the same 80/20 train-test split as the probe, and we report test $R^2$.

\subsection{Construction of belief states}
\subsubsection{Motivation and focus on layer 0}
The preceding sections provided evidence that belief states are linearly present in the residual stream and causally relevant to the model's output. A natural next question is how the model constructs these representations from the input. Piotrowski et al.~\cite{piotrowski2025constrained} recently proposed a theory of \emph{constrained belief updating} that answers this question for single-layer transformers trained on Mess3. According to this theory, the attention mechanism implements a parallelized approximation to Bayesian inference, producing a characteristic intermediate geometry, called the \textit{constrained belief geometry}, that the MLP then refines into the full belief state. We test whether the same mechanism operates in Llama's first attention layer (layer 0). We focus on this layer because probing reveals, as we will show, that the belief geometry already emerges there. Specifically, in layer 0, the residual stream activations transition from three token-identity clusters at \texttt{resid\_pre} to a full belief geometry at \texttt{resid\_post} (post-attention, post-MLP).

\subsubsection{HMM parameters}
For the constrained belief experiments, we use the Mess3 parameters from Piotrowski et al. \cite{piotrowski2025constrained}: $\alpha = 0.6$, $x = 0.15$. This gives an eigenvalue $\zeta = 1 - 3x = 0.55$ of the transition matrix. Using this parameterization, the constrained and full Bayesian belief geometries are visually distinct: the constrained belief geometry exhibits a straight Sierpinski-like fractal edges, while the full Bayesian geometry has curved edges. We generate a single 2000-token sequence and discard the first 250 positions as burn-in, since the probe results above suggest that the model learns the geometry at or past 250 tokens.

\subsubsection{Theoretical predictions}
\label{sec:constrained_theory}
Piotrowski et al.\ show that in a single-layer transformer, the attention mechanism decomposes the constrained belief update (their Eq.~5) into per-source contributions. Each source position~$s$ contributes a correction to the belief at destination position~$d$ of the form $\zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$, where $\mathbf{d}_{z_s} = \boldsymbol{\pi} T^{|z_s} - \boldsymbol{\pi}$ is a direction in belief space determined by the source token (one of three possible directions), and $\zeta^{d-s}$ is a scalar that decays exponentially with token distance. This leads to four testable predictions about the residual stream at each stage:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Token embeddings} (\texttt{resid\_pre}) should map linearly to $\boldsymbol{\pi} T^{|z_d} - \boldsymbol{\pi}$: the current token's belief-space direction (Eqs.~14--15).
\item \textbf{Attention output} (\texttt{attn\_out}) should map linearly to $\sum_{s < d} \zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$: the exponentially-weighted sum of past token directions (from Eq.~5).
\item \textbf{Post-attention activations} (\texttt{resid\_mid}) should map to the constrained belief state (Eq.~5): $\boldsymbol{\pi} + \sum_{s=1}^{d} \zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$.
\item \textbf{Post-MLP activations} (\texttt{resid\_post}) should map to the full Bayesian belief state (Eq.~2).
\end{enumerate}

 \subsubsection{Effective weight for multi-head attention}
\label{sec:effective_weight}
Prediction~2 states that \texttt{attn\_out} should map to $\sum_{s < d} \zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$. Probing tests whether this holds for the total sum. But the sum also makes a stronger prediction. It is composed of one term per past token $s$, each decaying as $\zeta^{d-s}$ with distance, where $\zeta$ is predetermined by the HMM parameters. This means that the impact of a token on the belief state should decrease exponentially the further back the token is from the current position. Accordingly, contributions based on token distance in \texttt{attn\_out} should scale by $\zeta$. 

In a single-head transformer, each per-source contribution is a scalar attention weight $A(d,s)$ times an OV vector, and since the OV vector depends only on the source token (not on distance), the distance dependence is proportional to $A(d,s)$. One can plot $A(d,s)$ by distance and observe exponential decay (Piotrowski et al., Figure~2C). With 24 heads, this is no longer possible because, for each (destination, source) pair there are 24 scalar weights, each paired with a different OV circuit, and there is no single number to plot. We derive what we call an \textit{effective weight} that collapses these into one scalar per (destination, source) pair.
 
The total contribution from source token~$s$ to destination token~$d$ across all heads is
\begin{equation}
\text{contribution}(d, s) = \sum_{h=1}^{24} A_h(d,s) \cdot \mathbf{v}_h(s) \quad \in \mathbb{R}^{3072}\,,
\end{equation}
where $A_h(d,s)$ is the attention weight and $\mathbf{v}_h(s) = W_O^{(h)} W_V^{(h)} \mathbf{x}_s$ is the OV vector of head~$h$ at position~$s$. The theory predicts that, projected into belief space via a linear map~$W$, this should equal $\zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$:
\begin{equation}
\text{contribution}(d,s) \cdot W = c \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}\,,
\label{eq:effective_weight_setup}
\end{equation}
where $c = \zeta^{d-s}$ is the unknown scalar. Taking the dot product of both sides with $\mathbf{d}_{z_s}$:
\begin{equation}
\big(\text{contribution}(d,s) \cdot W\big) \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s} = c \cdot \|\mathbf{d}_{z_s}\|^2\,,
\end{equation}
and solving gives what we call the \emph{effective weight}:
\begin{equation}
c = \frac{\big(\text{contribution}(d,s) \cdot W\big) \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}}{\|\mathbf{d}_{z_s}\|^2}\,.
\label{eq:effective_weight}
\end{equation}
This scalar measures how much source~$s$ pushed the belief at destination~$d$ along the direction predicted by the theory. The dot product discards any component perpendicular to $\mathbf{d}_{z_s}$.

The map $W$ is the only unknown in this equation. Estimating it is non-trivial. Here, we estimate it using linear probes, and show converging results using two types of probes. \textbf{Probe~A} (attn\_out $\to$ expected attention output) provides the most targeted projection, but its target contains $\zeta^k$ explicitly. This raises a circularity concern: if $W$ was trained to find a subspace where the total matches a $\zeta^k$-weighted sum, could good matches to $\zeta$ simply emerge from this fact? \textbf{Probe~B} (attn\_out $\to$ full Bayesian beliefs) mitigates this, since its target is computed by the full Bayesian update with no $\zeta$ in the formula.

Both of our probes are learned without a bias term. This is because each per-source contribution should map to $\zeta^{d-s} \cdot \mathbf{d}_{z_s}$, which goes to zero as $d - s \to \infty$, and a map with a bias $b$ would send negligible contributions to $b$ rather than zero. Empirically, adding a bias improves $R^2$ by only 0.0006.
 
\subsubsection{Probe-free validation}
\label{sec:probefree}
Any learned projection $W$ selects 3 directions out of 3072 and could in principle reveal spurious patterns along those directions. To verify the decay without any learned map, we use two methods on the raw $\mathbb{R}^{3072}$ attention output vectors:
 
\paragraph{PCA and coloring.} We apply PCA to attn\_out across all post-burn-in positions and color each point by the identity of the token at lag~$k$, for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ If the attention output encodes token history in a hierarchically nested structure, coloring by $t{-}1$ should separate the major clusters, coloring by $t{-}2$ should separate sub-clusters, and so on.
 
\paragraph{Between-group variance.} For each lag~$k$, we group all attn\_out vectors by the token at $t{-}k$ and compute the between-group variance (the fraction of total variance attributable to the lag-$k$ token identity). If the effective weighting of past tokens decays as $\zeta^k$, the between-group variance should decay as $\zeta^{2k}$ (since variance scales with squared displacement). We fit a log-linear model to lags $k = 1$ through $k = 6$ and extract the implied~$\zeta$.

\section{Preliminary Results}

\subsection{In-context HMM prediction}

The model converges to near-Bayesian-optimal predictions after a short initial warm-up. Across 30 Mess3 sequences, the mean convergence position is 22.6 tokens ($\sigma = 4.0$). The KL divergence decays smoothly over the first 20--30 tokens and remains at or near the threshold for the remainder of the 5,000-token sequence. Figure~\ref{fig:kl_over_sequence} shows a representative example.

Light blue: raw per-token KL; dark blue: smoothed KL (window $w=5$). The red dashed line indicates the convergence threshold (2\% of maximum KL). The green dashed lines mark the earliest allowed position (min\_position = 20) and the crossing point $t^* = 22$.}

\subsection{Linear decodability of belief states}

\paragraph{Probe performance.}
Linear probes trained on pooled activations achieve high $R^2$ across most layers (Figure~\ref{fig:r2_per_layer}). Performance peaks at layers 2--4, where the eval $R^2$ reaches approximately 0.999 (MSE $\approx 1.8 \times 10^{-4}$). Layer 0 is notably worse ($R^2 \approx 0.988$), and performance degrades at the final layers, reaching $R^2 \approx 0.985$ at layer 27. All probes are evaluated on held-out sequences that were not seen during training; the small gap between train and eval performance indicates that the probes generalize well.

\paragraph{Cross-sequence consistency.}
Table~\ref{tab:cross_seq} reports pairwise probe similarity metrics across sequences at selected layers, using only post-convergence activations. At early layers, probes trained on different sequences recover nearly identical directions: at layer~0, the mean pairwise cosine similarity is 0.89 and the first principal angle $\theta_1$ is $23^\circ$, with a cross-MSE ratio near~1 (applying one sequence's probe to another's activations incurs almost no additional error). Probe directions diverge monotonically with depth: by layer~15, cosine similarity has dropped to 0.51 and $\theta_1$ has risen to $54^\circ$; by layer~27, cosine is 0.18 and $\theta_1$ reaches $75^\circ$ (near-orthogonal). The cross-MSE ratio peaks around layers 12--15 (${\sim}1.3$), where probes are still accurate individually but have diverged enough in direction that cross-application incurs meaningful additional error; at later layers the ratio decreases again because all probes, including self-evaluated ones, perform worse. This pattern indicates that the belief-state encoding is most canonical---shared across contexts---at the early layers where probe $R^2$ is also highest.

\paragraph{Emergence.}
Figure~\ref{fig:lag_summary} shows, for each layer, the mean lag between the token position at which the probe's $R^2$ crosses 0.9 and the token position at which the model's output KL converges. For layers 0--6, this lag is negative: for example, at layer 3, the probe threshold is reached approximately 6.3 tokens before the KL convergence point on average. At layer 10, the lag is approximately zero. At layers 15 and later, the probe trails the KL convergence by several tokens, reaching up to 26 tokens of lag at layer 27. This pattern is consistent with a computational pipeline in which the belief state is first assembled in a clean linear form at early layers, before the model's output distribution has converged, and is then transformed by later layers into a format that is less linearly decodable but more directly useful for output generation (see tuned lens results below). However, the lag at later layers may also partly reflect the lower cross-sequence consistency of those representations (Table~\ref{tab:cross_seq}): because the pooled probe's direction is a poorer match for any individual sequence at later layers, the $R^2$ threshold is reached later even if the information is present in a sequence-specific subspace.

\subsection{Causal use of belief states}

\paragraph{Activation patching.}
In the unpatched baseline, the model's output distribution is close to the factual Bayesian-optimal predictions (mean KL = 0.030, $\sigma = 0.035$) and substantially further from the counterfactual (mean KL = 0.397, $\sigma = 0.166$). Figure~\ref{fig:crossing} shows the crossing plot: as the patching layer increases, the model's KL to the counterfactual decreases and its KL to the factual increases, consistent with the patched activations carrying information that shifts the model's beliefs toward the counterfactual. The effect is concentrated in the early-to-mid layers, broadly consistent with the probe results.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{figures/crossing_plot.png}
  \caption{Crossing plot for activation patching. The $y$-axis shows mean KL divergence to the factual (blue) and counterfactual (orange) Bayesian-optimal distributions, as a function of the layer at which patching is applied. The crossover point indicates the layer at which the patched activations carry sufficient belief state information to shift the model's predictions toward the counterfactual.}
  \label{fig:crossing}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Factual--counterfactual discrepancy and token probability.}
Although the factual and counterfactual conditions share an identical prefix and differ only in the final token, counterfactual patches consistently yield higher KL to their target than factual patches. This is initially surprising, since attention at the intervention position draws on the same context in both cases.

To investigate, we designed a control experiment that crosses two binary factors: whether the patch is \emph{factual} (the variant sequence ends in the token whose belief is being injected) or \emph{counterfactual} (it ends in a different token), and whether the injected belief corresponds to a \emph{likely} or \emph{unlikely} next token under the prefix belief. For each base sequence, we generate all three variant sequences (one per possible final token) and all three target beliefs, yielding nine patch specs per sequence. A sanity check confirms that for a fixed target belief, patching KL is invariant across the three variant sequences (mean within-group standard deviation $\approx 0$), validating that causal masking isolates the intervention from the identity of the actual final token.

The main finding is that the factual--counterfactual distinction does not explain the KL gap. When we condition on token probability, the factual and counterfactual curves collapse: likely-token beliefs are reconstructed well regardless of whether the patch is factual or counterfactual, while unlikely-token beliefs show higher KL in both cases (Figure~\ref{fig:patching_token_prob}). The apparent factual advantage arises because, under the Mess3 parameterization, one token is typically much more probable than the other two at any given state, so factual patches disproportionately correspond to likely tokens.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{figures/patching_token_prob.png}
  \caption{Activation patching KL vs.\ layer, split by token probability. Left: factual patches; right: counterfactual patches. In both panels, likely-token beliefs (blue) yield lower KL than unlikely-token beliefs (orange). The two panels are nearly identical, confirming that the factual--counterfactual distinction is not the relevant factor; token probability is.}
  \label{fig:patching_token_prob}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Decoder fidelity.}
To confirm that the probability effect originates in the decoder rather than in the model's downstream computation, we measure decoder reconstruction error as a function of token probability $p_t = P(x_t \mid \mathbf{b}_{t-1})$. For each sequence position after a transient cutoff, we compute both the decoder-only MSE $\lVert \mathbf{a}_t - D(\mathbf{b}_t) \rVert^2$ (comparing the actual activation to the decoder's output given the true belief) and a round-trip MSE $\lVert \mathbf{a}_t - D(E(\mathbf{a}_t)) \rVert^2$ (encoding the activation and decoding it back). Both metrics show a negative correlation with $p_t$: belief states associated with unlikely tokens have systematically higher reconstruction error, particularly at early-to-mid layers where the patching effect is strongest (Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_fidelity}). This confirms that the decoders, trained on the natural distribution of Mess3 activations, are less faithful for beliefs corresponding to rare emissions---precisely the regime that dominates counterfactual patches.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/decoder_fidelity.png}
  \caption{Normalized decoder MSE ($\lVert \mathbf{a}_t - D(\mathbf{b}_t) \rVert^2 / \lVert \mathbf{a}_t \rVert^2$) vs.\ token probability $p_t$ at each layer. Each blue dot is one (sequence, position) pair; the red line shows the binned mean. At early-to-mid layers, low-probability tokens exhibit markedly higher reconstruction error, explaining why patching with beliefs corresponding to unlikely tokens yields worse results.}
  \label{fig:decoder_fidelity}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Balanced-decoder patching.}
To test whether the decoder fidelity issue accounts for the elevated KL in non-factual patching conditions, we retrain the encoder-decoder on a probability-balanced dataset: token positions are subsampled so that likely and unlikely emissions are equally represented. We then repeat the activation patching experiment with the balanced decoders. Averaged over layers 5--15 (where the patching effect is strongest), counterfactual KL drops by 32\% relative to the unbalanced decoders, garbage-valid by 26\%, and garbage-random by 29\%, while factual KL remains essentially unchanged ($< 3\%$ shift). The gap between factual and garbage-random conditions narrows by approximately 40\%. This confirms that a substantial fraction of the non-factual patching penalty originates in the decoder rather than in the model's downstream computation, though a residual gap persists even after balancing.

\paragraph{Activation steering.}
Steering successfully shifts the model's output distribution toward the target belief state across all conditions. Figure~\ref{fig:steering_kl} shows KL divergence to the target as a function of the intervention layer. In the unsteered baseline the model's output is already close to its factual optimum (mean KL~$= 0.029$, $\sigma = 0.031$); steering drives the output toward a \emph{different} target belief, and the KL to that target drops to comparable levels at the best-performing layers.

For the past-consistent conditions, steering at late layers (21--27) achieves KL to target below 0.03 for all tested values of $K$, approaching the unsteered baseline level. At the early-to-mid layers where linear probes are strongest (layers 2--4), steering also performs well: for $K = 1$, KL to target at layer~3 is $0.045 \pm 0.037$. Steering at middle layers (5--13) shows somewhat higher KL, consistent with the pattern seen in patching.

The shared-prefix and different-prefix variants of the past-consistent condition yield broadly similar KL-to-target curves across layers and $K$ values, with no systematic advantage for either variant. This suggests that prefix-attention conflict---where the source prefix is inconsistent with the injected donor trajectory---does not substantially degrade steering performance, and the belief-injection effect is the dominant factor.

The control conditions confirm the specificity of the intervention. Garbage-valid steering (target is a real Mess3 belief from an unrelated sequence) achieves KL to target of $0.136 \pm 0.096$ at layer~3, substantially worse than past-consistent steering at the same layer, but still converges to near-baseline levels at late layers. Garbage-random steering (target sampled uniformly from the simplex) follows a similar pattern, with consistently higher KL than the garbage-valid condition at early and mid layers.

These steering results were obtained with the same decoders used in the patching experiments, before the decoder fidelity issue described above was identified. The elevated KL at middle layers (roughly 6--17) may therefore reflect the same decoder bias toward likely-token beliefs rather than a genuine limitation of the steering intervention. Whether this mid-layer KL gap persists once decoders are retrained on a probability-balanced dataset remains to be tested.

\paragraph{Tuned lens.}
The model-target tuned lens reveals a sharp improvement in per-layer predictions beginning around layers 13--14, after which KL divergence to the Bayesian-optimal distribution drops substantially. Strikingly, this improvement occurs precisely in the layers where linear probe $R^2$ is \emph{lower} than its early-layer peak (Figure~\ref{fig:r2_per_layer}). Early layers (2--4), where linear probes achieve their highest $R^2$, do not yield correspondingly better model-target tuned lens decoding. This dissociation suggests that the quality of the linear belief-state encoding is not the primary determinant of whether a layer's activations are sufficient to recover the correct output distribution.

However, the \emph{HMM-target} tuned lens tells a complementary story: when the translator is trained to decode toward the HMM's observation probabilities rather than the model's own output, KL(HMM $\|$ tuned\_hmm) is minimized at precisely the early layers (3--7) where probe $R^2$ peaks. This confirms that the belief state is most faithfully encoded at early layers; the model-target tuned lens's late-layer improvement instead reflects the accumulation of output-formatting computation that is orthogonal to the belief geometry. The later-layer computation analysis (Section~\ref{sec:later_layer}) corroborates this interpretation.

\paragraph{Later-layer computation.}
\label{sec:later_layer}
Decomposing activations into belief-aligned and orthogonal components reveals that belief-state variance decreases monotonically from $\sim$1--4\% at layer 0 to $\sim$0.1--0.2\% at layer 27, while orthogonal variance grows correspondingly. Yet decoders trained on the \emph{full} activation vector improve with depth ($R^2$: 0.955 at layer 0 $\to$ 0.984 at layer 27), while decoders restricted to the belief subspace remain poor ($R^2 \approx 0.2$). This means later layers encode prediction-relevant information \emph{orthogonal} to the belief subspace---performing output formatting and logit refinement rather than improving the belief representation itself. This ``compute early, format late'' pattern is consistent across Mess3 and Fern configurations.

\section{Discussion}

We organize our findings around the four questions posed in the introduction. For each, we list explicit claims and tag them by the strength of the supporting evidence: \emph{strong evidence} denotes claims backed by quantitative experiments with clear metrics and appropriate controls; \emph{suggestive evidence} denotes claims supported by indirect or preliminary results that admit alternative explanations. We also note open questions that remain for the second half of the project.

\subsection{Prediction quality}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Llama-3.2-3B predicts HMM sequences near-optimally in context} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  The model converges to near-Bayesian-optimal predictions within approximately 23 tokens across 30 Mess3 sequences and sustains them over 5{,}000 tokens (Figure~\ref{fig:kl_over_sequence}). This holds across multiple Mess3 parameterizations and the Fern process in the belief-state sweep (Table~\ref{tab:sweep_r2}).
\end{itemize}

Whether this generalizes to other model scales and what determines convergence speed remain open questions.

\subsection{Belief-state representation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{In-context belief states are linearly recoverable from the LLM residual stream} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  Pooled linear probes achieve $R^2 > 0.998$ at layers 2--4 for Mess3, generalizing to held-out sequences (Figure~\ref{fig:r2_per_layer}). Encoder-decoder $R^2$ exceeds 0.996 at layers 1--5. This holds across multiple Mess3 parameterizations and for the Fern process ($R^2 > 0.97$).

  \item \textbf{The model tracks genuine latent structure, not merely its output distribution} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  Fern has a non-injective emission matrix (3 hidden states, 2 output tokens), so the output distribution does not uniquely determine the belief state. Despite this, linear probes recover the full three-dimensional belief state with $R^2 > 0.97$ and the HMM-target tuned lens achieves $D_{\mathrm{KL}} < 0.0001$ at early layers (Table~\ref{tab:sweep_r2}). This rules out the hypothesis that the representation merely compresses output probabilities.

  \item \textbf{The belief-state encoding is most canonical at early layers and emerges before output convergence} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  Cross-sequence probe cosine similarity is 0.89 at layer~0 and declines monotonically to 0.18 at layer~27; the first principal angle between per-sequence probe weight matrices rises from $23^\circ$ to $75^\circ$ (Table~\ref{tab:cross_seq}). At layer~3, the probe $R^2$ threshold is reached approximately 6.3 tokens before the model's output KL converges (Figure~\ref{fig:lag_summary}).

  \item \textbf{The belief geometry becomes less linearly decodable with depth} \emph{(suggestive evidence).}
  $R^2$ drops from 0.999 at layers 2--4 to 0.985 at layer~27, cross-sequence consistency declines, and belief-state variance as a fraction of total activation variance shrinks from 1--4\% to 0.1--0.2\%. However, whether this reflects genuine degradation of the representation or a transformation into a format that is less amenable to linear readout but still informationally complete remains unresolved. Comparing the belief-state subspaces at different layers---which can be done with already-trained probes---would help disambiguate these possibilities.
\end{itemize}

Open questions remain regarding the Leopard process, which yields substantially lower probe $R^2$ (0.33--0.57). One possibility is that Leopard's quasi-deterministic transition structure causes the belief state to cover the simplex more uniformly over time, making it harder for linear probes to resolve nearby belief states even if the model tracks them internally; alternatively, the model may genuinely fail to form an accurate representation. Whether nonlinear probes would improve the picture is untested. A Fern-specific challenge is that the 2-token vocabulary produces higher junk mass than Mess3, which can inflate KL metrics. All results to date use Llama-3.2-3B; generalization across model scales has not been tested.

\subsection{Belief-state construction}

The mechanism by which the model constructs the belief geometry from context is the least understood aspect of the pipeline. The evidence here is preliminary.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The model may construct belief states through an additive heuristic approximating Bayesian updating} \emph{(suggestive evidence).}
  PCA of the aggregate attention output (summed across heads) at early layers reveals a fractal-like recursive structure: the token identity at position $t{-}1$ determines which region of the fractal is occupied, $t{-}2$ determines which sub-region, and so on. Between-group variance decreases exponentially with token distance (slope $\approx -0.55$), which is qualitatively compatible with the constrained belief update framework---an additive approximation of Bayesian updating in which each attention step incorporates one token's worth of evidence. The model appears to track sequence history up to approximately 6--7 tokens back, beyond which noise dominates. Probing MLP outputs for the correction vector between constrained and Bayesian-optimal belief states yields high $R^2$, suggesting that MLPs may contribute a correction on top of the attention-based additive update. However, the constrained belief update framework was derived for 1--2 layer transformers with 1--2 attention heads, and it is unclear whether the qualitative resemblance reflects genuine mechanistic similarity in a 28-layer, 24-head model. Attention outputs also show unexpected predictive power for the correction term, complicating the attribution between attention and MLP contributions.
\end{itemize}

Open questions include whether specific attention heads or MLPs can be identified as implementing belief construction, why the HMM-target tuned lens shows anomalously good performance at layer~0 despite weaker probe $R^2$ (suggesting a rough belief representation may already exist in the token embeddings), and more generally whether the model implements anything resembling Bayesian updating or relies on qualitatively different heuristics.

\subsection{Belief-to-output routing}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The belief representation is causally relevant to the model's output} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  Both activation patching and activation steering shift the model's output distribution toward a target belief state (Figures~\ref{fig:crossing},~\ref{fig:steering_kl}). The effect is systematic across factual, counterfactual, garbage-valid, and garbage-random conditions, confirming that the belief-state encoding is not merely an epiphenomenal correlate of the model's computation.

  \item \textbf{Token probability, not factual status, explains decoder-mediated intervention quality} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  The factual--counterfactual gap in patching is explained by token probability: conditioning on token probability collapses the two curves (Figure~\ref{fig:patching_token_prob}). This traces to decoder fidelity---decoders trained on the natural Mess3 distribution have higher reconstruction error for unlikely-token beliefs (Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_fidelity}). Retraining decoders on probability-balanced activations reduces the gap, further supporting this diagnosis.

  \item \textbf{Later layers perform output formatting orthogonal to the belief subspace} \emph{(strong evidence).}
  Full-activation decoders improve with depth ($R^2$: 0.955 at layer~0 to 0.984 at layer~27) while decoders restricted to the belief subspace remain poor ($R^2 \approx 0.2$). The HMM-target tuned lens achieves its best performance at early layers (3--7), confirming that the cleanest belief-state representation for prediction is at early layers (Figure~\ref{fig:tuned_lens_sweep}). The model-target tuned lens, by contrast, improves monotonically with depth, reflecting progressive convergence toward the model's final output format. Together, these indicate that later layers transform information into a format suitable for the unembedding rather than refining the belief representation itself.

  \item \textbf{Late layers implement a robust routing mechanism that tolerates noisy or injected beliefs} \emph{(suggestive evidence).}
  Patching and steering are most effective at late layers (roughly 14+), where even garbage beliefs sampled uniformly from the simplex can be routed to outputs achieving KL to target comparable to the unsteered baseline. Factual patching at late layers can match or improve over the unpatched baseline, possibly because the injected activation is less noisy than what propagates through the normal forward pass---though this interpretation has not been directly verified.
\end{itemize}

Open questions include what specific mechanisms in layers 14+ enable effective routing despite weaker linear belief encoding, whether ablating the belief subspace at various layers would affect downstream output, whether the remaining likely/unlikely-token gap persists after full decoder rebalancing, and whether the causal findings from Mess3 generalize to Fern and Leopard.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Taken together, the evidence supports the following tentative account of how Llama-3.2-3B processes HMM-generated sequences. In early layers (roughly 0--4), the model constructs a belief-state geometry from the recent context, possibly through an additive heuristic operating over the last 6--7 tokens. This geometry is linearly decodable, shared across sequences, and emerges before the model's output distribution has converged. As information propagates through mid layers, the belief geometry becomes less linearly prominent---whether through genuine degradation or transformation---while the model accumulates output-formatting computation in dimensions orthogonal to the belief subspace. At late layers (roughly 14+), a distinct routing mechanism maps whatever belief-relevant information is present to the output distribution, with sufficient robustness that even externally injected beliefs are faithfully transmitted.

This account remains speculative in its details: the three phases are inferred from the convergence of multiple experimental signals rather than from direct mechanistic analysis, and the boundaries between them are approximate. Establishing whether this pipeline accurately describes the model's computation, and identifying the specific circuits responsible for each phase, is the central goal for the remainder of the project.

\section{Next Steps}

The first half of the project has established that Llama-3.2-3B predicts HMM sequences near-optimally in context, that the belief-state geometry is linearly present in the residual stream, and that this geometry is causally relevant to the model's output. The second half shifts focus toward understanding the \emph{mechanisms} underlying belief-state construction and output routing---the two least understood phases of the pipeline described in the synthesis above.

\paragraph{Belief-state construction.}
The constrained belief update analysis has produced suggestive evidence that the model constructs beliefs through an additive heuristic operating over recent tokens, but the connection to the theoretical framework remains qualitative. Priorities include: identifying whether specific attention heads implement the additive update and whether specific MLPs implement the correction to Bayesian-optimal; testing whether the constrained belief update structure holds quantitatively (not just in PCA shape); and probing intermediate residual stream positions (e.g.\ resid-mid for constrained beliefs, resid-post for corrected beliefs) to more directly test the two-stage construction hypothesis.

\paragraph{Output routing.}
The late-layer routing mechanism is effective but uncharacterized. Ablation experiments that project out the belief-state subspace at various layers and measure the downstream effect on model output would determine whether later layers rely on the linear belief geometry or on information encoded in other dimensions. Extending the patching and steering pipeline to Fern and Leopard would test whether the causal findings from Mess3 generalize across processes with different emission geometries.

\paragraph{Representation geometry.}
Several targeted checks would sharpen the representational claims: comparing belief-state subspaces across layers (using already-trained probes) to determine whether the encoding rotates or genuinely degrades with depth; testing nonlinear probes on Leopard to distinguish probe failure from encoding failure; and characterizing what structural properties of an HMM (transition sparsity, mixing time, emission rank) predict encoding quality.

\paragraph{Scale.}
All results to date use a single model (Llama-3.2-3B). Running the core experiments---probing, tuned lens, and at least one causal intervention---on Llama-3.2-1B and a larger model would test whether the findings reflect general properties of transformer in-context learning or are specific to this model's capacity.
